# PART ONE: HISTORICAL AND THEOLOGICAL FOUNDATION

## 1.1 Patristic Origins

The fourfold sense of Scripture represents the mature synthesis of patristic exegetical method, developed through centuries of Christian engagement with sacred text. The foundational conviction underlying this method is that Scripture, inspired by the Holy Spirit, contains depths of meaning that exceed the immediate historical and grammatical sense while never contradicting it.

**Origen of Alexandria** (c. 185-254) provided the earliest systematic articulation of multiple senses, distinguishing between the bodily (literal), soulish (moral), and spiritual (mystical) dimensions of Scripture. His threefold schema corresponded to the anthropological trichotomy of body, soul, and spirit, grounding hermeneutical method in theological anthropology.

**John Cassian** (c. 360-435) transmitted to the Latin West the fourfold formulation that would become standard:

> *Littera gesta docet, quid credas allegoria,*
> *Moralis quid agas, quo tendas anagogia.*

The letter teaches events, allegory what you should believe, the moral sense what you should do, anagogy where you should aim.

**The Cappadocian Fathers**, particularly Gregory of Nyssa in his *Life of Moses*, demonstrated how narrative could simultaneously convey historical record, christological anticipation, moral formation, and eschatological orientation.

**Augustine of Hippo** (354-430) articulated the principle that Scripture teaches nothing contrary to faith and morals, establishing the rule of faith as the boundary within which spiritual interpretation operates. His *De Doctrina Christiana* provided hermeneutical theory; his *Enarrationes in Psalmos* provided extensive practical demonstration.

**Maximus the Confessor** (c. 580-662) developed the most sophisticated theological articulation of multiple senses, grounding them in the logoi that inhere in all created reality. For Maximus, the multiple senses of Scripture reflect the multiple dimensions of reality itself; to read Scripture at multiple levels is to perceive reality at multiple levels.

## 1.2 The Polyphonic Nature of Sacred Text

The fourfold sense is not an interpretive grid imposed upon Scripture from outside but rather the recognition of Scripture's inherent polyphonic nature. Just as a violin string sounding middle C simultaneously produces overtones at the octave, the fifth, and the third, so every passage of Scripture sounds its literal meaning while simultaneously producing allegorical, tropological, and anagogical resonance.

This polyphony reflects the dual authorship of Scripture. Human authors, guided by the Spirit, wrote with their own intentions, circumstances, and linguistic capacities. The Divine Author, inspiring the text, intended meanings that transcend while never contradicting human authorial intention. The Church's interpretive tradition, guided by the same Spirit who inspired the text, discerns the spiritual senses that emerge from and build upon the literal foundation.

## 1.3 The Principle of Christological Unity

The unity of Scripture across its multiple senses is christological. Christ is the interpretive key that unlocks all dimensions of meaning. He Himself established this hermeneutical principle on the road to Emmaus: "Beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he interpreted to them in all the Scriptures the things concerning himself" (Luke 24:27).

The allegorical sense discovers Christ prefigured in type and shadow. The tropological sense forms the reader in christological virtue. The anagogical sense points toward the consummation of all things in Christ. The literal sense narrates the history that reaches its climax in the Incarnation. All four senses find their coherence in the one Logos through whom all things were made and in whom all things hold together.

## 1.4 The Hermeneutical Circle

The four senses operate not in isolation but in hermeneutical circulation. The literal sense provides the foundation upon which spiritual senses rest; the spiritual senses illuminate dimensions of the literal sense that would otherwise remain hidden. Allegorical insight reveals christological depth in historical narrative; tropological application grounds moral formation in narrative encounter; anagogical orientation frames temporal events within eternal perspective.

This circulation means that reading at one level enhances reading at other levels. The reader who perceives Isaac as type of Christ reads the Akedah differently at the literal level. The reader who applies Abraham's obedience to their own moral life understands the allegorical dimension more deeply. The reader who perceives the eschatological horizon understands why typology and moral formation matter. The senses are not separate layers but interpenetrating dimensions of a single encounter with sacred text.

# PART TWO: THE LITERAL SENSE (SENSUS LITERALIS)

## 2.1 Definition and Scope

The literal sense (sensus literalis, sensus historicus) comprises what the text says at the level of grammar, history, and literary form. The literal sense answers:

- What events does this passage describe?
- What commands does it give?
- What propositions does it assert?
- What questions does it ask?
- What literary form does it employ?

The literal sense is not naive literalism. It encompasses the literary conventions, genres, and forms that govern meaning in ancient Israelite and Hellenistic contexts. A parable's literal sense is not that the events literally occurred but that the parabolic form communicates its intended meaning. Poetry's literal sense is not prosaic assertion but the meaning conveyed through poetic convention. The literal sense is what the human author intended to communicate through the literary form employed.

## 2.2 The Foundation of All Other Senses

The literal sense provides the foundation upon which all spiritual senses rest. This foundational priority is not merely methodological but theological. The God who reveals Himself in Scripture is the God who acts in history; the events recorded literally occurred (where the genre indicates historical report); the words spoken were actually spoken; the commands given were actually given.

**The Principle of Non-Contradiction**: No spiritual interpretation may contradict what the passage literally says. The allegorical reading of Isaac as type of Christ does not deny that Isaac was a real person who really was bound on a real mountain. The tropological application of Abraham's obedience does not suggest that the events did not occur. The anagogical orientation toward heavenly realities does not render earthly events illusory. The spiritual senses emerge from and remain consonant with the literal sense.

**The Constraint of the Literal**: When patristic interpreters offer multiple spiritual readings of a passage, all such readings are constrained by the literal sense. Readings that violate the text's plain meaning, that require denying what the text clearly asserts, that depend on ignoring the grammatical or historical sense, are rejected as illegitimate. The Fathers were unanimous: the letter is the foundation; remove the foundation and the building collapses.

## 2.3 Minimum Activation Threshold

In the ΒΊΒΛΟΣ ΛΌΓΟΥ system, the literal sense maintains a minimum forty percent activation regardless of other sense distributions. This non-negotiable floor ensures followability. Even in passages with maximum allegorical or anagogical saturation, the reader must always know:

- Who is acting
- What is happening
- Where and when events occur
- How events unfold

If the spiritual senses overwhelm the literal, the reader becomes confused rather than formed. The surface narrative must remain coherent. The reader who misses the subliminal dimensions of the text must still experience satisfying narrative.

## 2.4 Literary Form and Literal Meaning

The literal sense requires sensitivity to literary form:

**Narrative**: The literal sense of narrative is what happened. Character, setting, plot, conflict, resolution—these elements constitute the literal meaning of narrative passages.

**Law**: The literal sense of legal material is what is commanded or forbidden, along with the conditions and consequences specified.

**Prophecy**: The literal sense includes both prediction and proclamation. Prophetic oracles address contemporary situations while anticipating future fulfillment.

**Poetry**: The literal sense is conveyed through poetic conventions: parallelism, metaphor, imagery, rhythm. To read poetry literally is not to flatten it into prose but to receive the meaning that poetic form conveys.

**Wisdom**: The literal sense of wisdom literature is the insight or instruction communicated. Proverbs are not predictions but observations about the general order of reality.

**Apocalyptic**: The literal sense of apocalyptic includes symbolic meaning. The beast with seven heads is not literally a creature with seven heads but a symbolic representation whose literal sense is the reality symbolized.

**Epistle**: The literal sense is the argument, instruction, or exhortation communicated through the letter's rhetorical structure.

## 2.5 The Antiochene Contribution

The Antiochene school of exegesis, represented supremely by St. John Chrysostom, emphasized the foundational importance of the literal sense. Chrysostom's homilies demonstrate meticulous attention to vocabulary, grammar, syntax, and historical context before drawing moral and spiritual applications.

The Antiochene emphasis on θεωρία (theoria), the capacity to perceive spiritual meaning within historical particularity, preserved the literal sense while opening to deeper dimensions. Theoria is not allegory that leaves history behind but insight that perceives the spiritual significance of historical events precisely as historical. Isaac is a type of Christ not despite but because of the particular historical circumstances of the Akedah.

# PART THREE: THE ALLEGORICAL SENSE (SENSUS ALLEGORICUS)

## 3.1 Definition and Scope

The allegorical sense (sensus allegoricus, sensus typologicus) comprises the christological and ecclesiological meaning. The allegorical sense answers:

- How does this passage point to Christ?
- What types and shadows find their fulfillment in Gospel realities?
- How does this passage illuminate the mystery of the Church?
- What correspondence exists between Old Testament events and New Testament fulfillment?

The allegorical sense depends upon the conviction that all Scripture speaks of Christ. The Old Testament is not merely preparation for or prediction of Christ but participation in the mystery that finds its full manifestation in His Incarnation, death, and resurrection.

## 3.2 Typological Structure

Typology operates through correspondence between earlier events, persons, or institutions (types) and their later fulfillments (antitypes). The correspondence is not arbitrary but grounded in the unity of salvation history and the consistency of divine action.

**Characteristics of Authentic Typology**:

1. **Historical Reality**: Both type and antitype are historically real. Isaac really was bound; Christ really was crucified. The typological correspondence does not require allegorizing away historical reality.

2. **Divine Design**: The correspondence is not coincidence but providence. God ordered salvation history so that earlier events would prefigure later fulfillment.

3. **Escalation**: The antitype exceeds the type. Christ's sacrifice exceeds Isaac's near-sacrifice; the true Passover Lamb exceeds the Egyptian Passover; the heavenly temple exceeds the earthly tabernacle.

4. **Patristic Attestation**: Authentic typological readings have patristic support. Novel connections unsupported by the Tradition require extreme caution.

## 3.3 Temporal Folding and Lexical Consistency

The ΒΊΒΛΟΣ ΛΌΓΟΥ system implements allegorical connections through temporal folding: the use of identical vocabulary at type and antitype locations across vast textual distances. When Isaac carries wood up Moriah, the vocabulary used must match exactly the vocabulary used when Christ carries the Cross up Golgotha.

This lexical consistency enables subliminal recognition. The reader's brain fires the same neural patterns at both encounters, creating felt connection without conscious analysis. The reader need not consciously think "Isaac prefigures Christ"; the neural pathway established by identical sensory vocabulary creates the connection below conscious awareness.

**Key Temporal Folding Phrases**:

| Phrase | Type Location | Antitype Location | Distance |
|--------|--------------|------------------|----------|
| "Silence of the lamb" | Abel's offering, Passover | Isaiah 53, Passion | 1800+ pages |
| "Weight of wood on shoulder" | Genesis 22 (Isaac) | Passion narrative | 2000+ pages |
| "Blood and water" | Creation waters | John 19 (pierced side) | 2100+ pages |
| "Bread broken" | Exodus (manna) | Last Supper, Emmaus | 1700+ pages |

## 3.4 Christological Reading Protocol

When approaching any Old Testament passage for allegorical reading:

1. **Identify Christological Themes**: What aspects of Christ's person or work might this passage illuminate?

2. **Search for Patristic Precedent**: How have the Fathers read this passage christologically?

3. **Locate Type-Antitype Correspondence**: What specific elements of the passage find fulfillment in Gospel narrative?

4. **Deploy Temporal Folding Vocabulary**: Use identical vocabulary at type and antitype locations.

5. **Maintain Literal Foundation**: The allegorical reading must not contradict or eliminate the literal sense.

## 3.5 Ecclesiological Extension

The allegorical sense extends beyond individual christology to include the mystery of the Church, the Body of Christ. Israel in the wilderness prefigures the Church in pilgrimage. The Tabernacle prefigures the Temple of living stones. The marriage of God and Israel prefigures Christ and His Bride.

Ecclesiological allegory follows the same principles as christological allegory: grounding in historical reality, divine design, escalation from type to antitype, and patristic attestation. The Church is not a replacement for Israel but Israel's fulfillment in the Messiah who incorporates both Jew and Gentile into one Body.

# PART FOUR: THE TROPOLOGICAL SENSE (SENSUS TROPOLOGICUS)

## 4.1 Definition and Scope

The tropological sense (sensus tropologicus, sensus moralis) comprises the moral and formational meaning. The tropological sense answers:

- How does this passage shape the reader's soul?
- What ethical transformation does it demand?
- What virtues does it cultivate?
- What vices does it correct?
- How should this passage affect the reader's conduct?

The tropological sense concerns spiritual formation through reading. Scripture does not merely teach morality; it forms moral character through narrative identification, embodied simulation, and vicarious experience.

## 4.2 Formation Through Narrative Identification

The tropological sense operates primarily through narrative identification rather than explicit moral teaching. Abraham's obedience becomes the template for the reader's obedience. David's repentance becomes the pattern for the reader's repentance. Ruth's loyalty becomes the model for the reader's loyalty.

**The Mechanism of Embodied Simulation**: When readers encounter Abraham raising the knife, their motor cortex activates as if they themselves were raising a knife. When readers encounter Christ's thirst on the Cross, their insular cortex activates as if they themselves were thirsty. This neural mirroring creates moral formation through vicarious experience.

The tropological sense achieves its effect through narrative immersion rather than explicit moral teaching. The prose does not say "and Abraham's obedience teaches us to obey God"—such explicit moralization would destroy the tropological operation. Instead, the prose renders Abraham's obedience with such vivid particularity that the reader experiences the obedience directly.

## 4.3 Virtue and Vice Framework

The tropological sense engages the classical virtue-vice framework:

**Virtues Cultivated Through Scripture**:
- Faith (πίστις): Trust in God's promises against appearance
- Hope (ἐλπίς): Confident expectation of divine fulfillment
- Love (ἀγάπη): Self-giving commitment to God and neighbor
- Prudence (φρόνησις): Practical wisdom in applying principles
- Justice (δικαιοσύνη): Rendering to each what is due
- Temperance (σωφροσύνη): Moderation and self-control
- Fortitude (ἀνδρεία): Courage in the face of difficulty

**Vices Corrected Through Scripture**:
- Unbelief: Failure to trust God's word
- Despair: Loss of confidence in divine mercy
- Self-love: Self-centered focus rather than love directed toward God and neighbor
- Folly: Failure to perceive truth
- Injustice: Violation of proper order
- Excess: Lack of restraint
- Cowardice: Failure of courage

## 4.4 The Theosis Framework

Orthodox tropological interpretation is oriented toward theosis (θέωσις), the progressive divinization of the human person. Scripture forms readers not merely toward ethical behavior but toward participation in divine nature.

**The Three Stages**:

1. **Κάθαρσις (Purification)**: Scripture addresses the passions, corrects disordered desire, cleanses from sin. Tropological reading at this stage emphasizes repentance, ascetic practice, and moral rectification.

2. **Φωτισμός (Illumination)**: Scripture illuminates the intellect with divine truth. Tropological reading at this stage emphasizes growing insight, the acquisition of wisdom, and the transformation of understanding.

3. **Θέωσις (Deification)**: Scripture participates in the union of human and divine. Tropological reading at this stage emphasizes loving union with God, the indwelling of Christ, and participation in divine life.

## 4.5 Wisdom Literature and Epistolary Material

Certain biblical genres carry elevated tropological weight by their very nature:

**Wisdom Literature** (Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, Sirach): These texts directly address the reader's conduct and character. Tropological activation reaches seventy to eighty percent in wisdom passages.

**Epistolary Material** (Paul's ethical instructions, James's practical teaching): Letters directly instruct the reader in Christian living. Tropological activation reaches thirty to forty percent in epistolary material, higher in explicitly ethical sections.

# PART FIVE: THE ANAGOGICAL SENSE (SENSUS ANAGOGICUS)

## 5.1 Definition and Scope

The anagogical sense (sensus anagogicus) comprises the eschatological and heavenly meaning. The anagogical sense answers:

- How does this passage point to eternal realities?
- What final consummation does it anticipate?
- How do present particulars participate in the eternal pattern?
- What heavenly reality does this earthly reality shadow?

The anagogical sense lifts every temporal event into the light of eternity, revealing how time participates in timelessness. The Jerusalem below becomes the Jerusalem above. The temple of stones becomes the temple of living stones. The earthly liturgy participates in the heavenly liturgy.

## 5.2 Eschatological Orientation

The anagogical sense trains readers to perceive present realities as icons of eternal realities. This perception is not escapism from earthly responsibility but the transformation of earthly activity through eternal perspective.

**Eschatological Dimensions**:

1. **Resurrection**: Physical death is not final; bodies will be raised; creation will be renewed.

2. **Judgment**: All human action is weighed; justice will be established; the righteous will be vindicated.

3. **Consummation**: History moves toward completion; God's purposes will be fulfilled; the Kingdom will be established in fullness.

4. **Eternal Life**: Union with God transcends temporal categories; theosis continues into eternity; the blessed participate in divine life forever.

## 5.3 Heavenly Correspondence

The anagogical sense perceives correspondence between earthly and heavenly realities:

| Earthly Reality | Heavenly Correspondence |
|----------------|------------------------|
| Jerusalem | Heavenly Jerusalem (Revelation 21) |
| Temple | Heavenly Temple (Hebrews 8-9) |
| Liturgy | Heavenly Liturgy (Revelation 4-5) |
| Sabbath | Eternal Rest (Hebrews 4) |
| Marriage | Christ and Church (Ephesians 5) |
| Kingdom | Kingdom of Heaven (Gospels throughout) |

## 5.4 Apocalyptic Literature

Apocalyptic literature (Daniel, Revelation) carries maximum anagogical weight. These texts directly address the eschatological horizon and the heavenly realm. For apocalyptic passages, anagogical activation reaches fifty to sixty percent while the literal sense must maintain sufficient clarity to preserve narrative coherence.

**Characteristics of Anagogically Saturated Prose**:

- Vocabulary carries eternal resonance
- Syntax creates openings toward infinity
- Imagery points beyond itself toward realities that exceed verbal containment
- The reader finishes with the sense that something beyond the words has been indicated

## 5.5 The Already/Not Yet Structure

Orthodox eschatology maintains the tension between the already and the not yet. The Kingdom has already arrived in Christ; the Kingdom has not yet been consummated in fullness. The anagogical sense holds this tension:

- Present realities already participate in heavenly patterns
- Present realities do not yet fully manifest heavenly glory
- The reader is trained to perceive both dimensions simultaneously

This tension prevents both escapist spiritualization (ignoring the literal sense in favor of pure heavenly meaning) and immanentist flattening (reducing everything to the present historical dimension without eschatological horizon).

## 6.3 Adjustment Principles

The default weightings may be adjusted based on specific passage characteristics:

**Elevating Literal Sense**: When the passage concerns foundational events (Creation, Exodus, Incarnation, Crucifixion), literal sense may be elevated to ensure clarity of what occurred.

**Elevating Allegorical Sense**: When the passage contains explicit typology or when the New Testament quotes the passage christologically, allegorical sense may be elevated.

**Elevating Tropological Sense**: When the passage directly addresses moral conduct or when character identification is particularly strong, tropological sense may be elevated.

**Elevating Anagogical Sense**: When the passage concerns eschatological themes or when heavenly realities are explicitly referenced, anagogical sense may be elevated.

**Constraint**: The literal sense must never fall below forty percent. This floor ensures followability regardless of how other senses are weighted.

# PART SEVEN: SENSE COLLISION PREVENTION

## 7.1 The Problem of Collision

When multiple senses approach maximum activation simultaneously, collision occurs. The reader's processing becomes confused, unable to determine which interpretive mode to employ. The reader experiences cognitive overload rather than formation.

**Symptoms of Collision**:
- The reader cannot follow the narrative
- The prose feels overwrought or heavy
- Symbolic meanings compete rather than complement
- The passage exhausts rather than transports

## 7.2 Hierarchical Constraint

The system prevents collision through hierarchical constraint:

**First Constraint**: The literal sense always maintains minimum forty percent activation. This floor is non-negotiable.

**Second Constraint**: The dominant sense for the passage type takes priority; other senses modulate to accommodate.

**Third Constraint**: Maximum combined activation varies by passage type:

| Passage Type | Maximum Combined Activation |
|-------------|---------------------------|
| Standard Narrative | 120% |
| Typological Narrative | 140% |
| Moral Instruction | 150% |
| Apocalyptic Vision | 160% |

## 7.3 The Functorial Architecture

The four senses can be formalized as categories (L, A, T, An) where objects are passages and morphisms represent valid interpretive transitions. Functors map passages from one interpretive register to another, preserving structural relationships.

**The Commutativity Requirement**: Arriving at tropological meaning from literal foundation through allegorical mediation must produce the same result as arriving through direct literal to tropological transition. When a passage violates commutativity, the functorial failure signals architectural unsoundness requiring revision.

**Formal Expression** (where ∘ denotes function composition, meaning the sequential application of functions):

Let f: L → A be the allegorical functor (moving from literal to allegorical sense)
Let g: A → T be the tropological functor via allegory (moving from allegorical to tropological sense)
Let h: L → T be the direct tropological functor (moving directly from literal to tropological sense)

The requirement is: g ∘ f = h (applying f then g must yield the same result as applying h directly)

If the path through allegory yields different tropological meaning than the direct path, revision is required.

# PART EIGHT: INTEGRATION PROTOCOLS

## 8.1 Pre-Composition Protocol

Before composing any passage:

1. **Identify Book Category**: Determine which of the six categories the passage belongs to.

2. **Apply Default Weighting**: Begin with the default percentages for that category.

3. **Assess Passage-Specific Factors**: Determine if adjustments are warranted based on content.

4. **Verify Literal Floor**: Confirm that literal sense will not fall below forty percent.

5. **Calculate Combined Activation**: Ensure total does not exceed category-appropriate maximum.

6. **Identify Potential Collisions**: Note where multiple senses might compete.

## 8.2 During Composition Protocol

While composing:

1. **Maintain Narrative Clarity**: The reader must always know what is happening at the literal level.

2. **Deploy Temporal Folding Vocabulary**: Use established vocabulary when type-antitype connections apply.

3. **Embed Tropological Formation**: Create conditions for reader identification without explicit moralization.

4. **Signal Anagogical Orientation**: Use vocabulary and imagery that points beyond the immediate.

5. **Monitor Density**: Avoid overcrowding any fifty-word span with excessive symbolic weight.

## 8.3 Post-Composition Verification

After composing:

1. **Verify Literal Coherence**: Read the passage at the literal level only. Is it followable?

2. **Verify Allegorical Integrity**: Check all typological connections for consistency.

3. **Verify Tropological Effect**: Does the passage create conditions for moral formation?

4. **Verify Anagogical Orientation**: Does the passage point beyond itself appropriately?

5. **Check for Collision**: Are there points where senses compete confusingly?

6. **Confirm Weighting**: Do the actual proportions match the intended proportions?

# PART NINE: VERIFICATION METHODS

## 9.1 Weighting Estimation

To estimate sense weighting in a composed passage:

**Literal Sense Markers**:
- Narrative action verbs
- Temporal and spatial markers
- Character names and dialogue
- Concrete sensory description
- Historical reference

**Allegorical Sense Markers**:
- Typological vocabulary
- Christological imagery
- Ecclesial reference
- Fulfillment language
- Type-antitype correspondence

**Tropological Sense Markers**:
- Virtue/vice vocabulary
- Emotional identification
- Moral choice narration
- Character formation
- Practical implication

**Anagogical Sense Markers**:
- Eschatological vocabulary
- Heavenly imagery
- Eternal reference
- Consummation language
- Transcendence gestures

## 9.2 Verification Algorithm

**Algorithm: FOURFOLD_VERIFY**

Input: Text passage P, intended weighting W
Output: Verification score and recommendations

1. Extract all literal sense markers from P
2. Extract all allegorical sense markers from P
3. Extract all tropological sense markers from P
4. Extract all anagogical sense markers from P
5. Calculate actual proportions based on marker density
6. Compare actual proportions to intended weighting W
7. If deviation exceeds 10% for any sense, flag for review
8. If literal sense falls below 40%, flag critical failure
9. Check for collision points (multiple senses above 80% in same span)
10. Return verification score and specific recommendations

## 9.3 Reader Response Testing

Effective fourfold integration can be tested through reader response:

**Literal Comprehension**: After reading, can the reader summarize what happened?

**Allegorical Perception**: Does the reader perceive christological connections without being told?

**Tropological Effect**: Does the reader feel moral challenge or formation?

**Anagogical Awareness**: Does the reader sense orientation toward eternal realities?

Optimal response pattern:
- Literal: 90%+ comprehension
- Allegorical: 60-70% perception (subliminal operation)
- Tropological: 70-80% effect (felt formation)
- Anagogical: 50-60% awareness (appropriate difficulty)

# PART TEN: WORKED EXAMPLES

## 10.1 Example One: Genesis 22 (The Akedah)

**Category**: Pentateuch
**Default Weighting**: L 55%, A 20%, T 18%, An 7%
**Adjustment**: Allegorical elevated to 30% due to explicit typology; Literal adjusted to 45%
**Final Weighting**: L 45%, A 30%, T 18%, An 7%

**Literal Sense Implementation**:
- Abraham's journey narrated with geographical specificity
- Isaac's question rendered with psychological realism
- The ram's appearance described concretely
- Dialogue presented with appropriate weight

**Allegorical Sense Implementation**:
- "Wood on his shoulder" vocabulary matched to Passion vocabulary
- "Only son" language echoes Johannine christology
- Mount Moriah connected to Temple Mount and Golgotha
- Substitutionary ram anticipates Lamb of God

**Tropological Sense Implementation**:
- Abraham's internal experience rendered to enable identification
- The test of faith becomes template for reader's own testing
- Obedience despite cost modeled for reader formation
- Trust in divine provision cultivated through narrative

**Anagogical Sense Implementation**:
- Abraham's confidence in resurrection hinted (Hebrews 11:19)
- The mountain of the Lord becomes site of eschatological provision
- The blessing extends to "all nations" in eschatological scope

## 10.2 Example Two: Psalm 23

**Category**: Wisdom/Poetic
**Default Weighting**: L 15%, A 2%, T 75%, An 8%
**No Adjustment Warranted**
